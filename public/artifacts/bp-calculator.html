Pediatric Blood Pressure Classifier and Management Guide based on AAP 2017 guidelines

Pediatric BP classifier

AAP 2017 guidelines · ages 1–17

Not required for age ≥13. Use growth chart estimate if needed.

Follow-up by stage
🟡 Elevated BP (90th–<95th %ile or 120–129/<80 in ≥13 yr)
Visit 1Confirm manually (2 readings). Begin lifestyle counseling. Recheck in 6 months.
Visit 2 (6 mo)Still elevated → reinforce lifestyle, review BMI/sleep/family hx. No labs yet unless red flags.
Visit 3 (12 mo)Persistent → obtain ABPM. If confirmed, reclassify as Stage 1. If normal → resume annual screening.
🟠 Stage 1 HTN (95th to 95th+12 mmHg or 130–139/80–89)
Visit 1Confirm manually, average 2 readings. Lifestyle counseling. Recheck in 1–2 weeks.
Visit 2Still Stage 1 → check BPs in both arms + 1 leg (r/o coarctation). Review meds, diet, sleep, FHx. Recheck in 3 months.
Persistent (3 visits)ABPM + workup: UA, BUN/Cr, electrolytes, lipids ± renal US, echo for LVH. Continue lifestyle ×6 mo; start meds if persistent or LVH/CKD/DM.
🔴 Stage 2 HTN (≥95th+12 mmHg or ≥140/90)
ImmediateConfirm manually. Measure both arms + 1 leg. Assess for symptoms (HA, vision, chest pain, neuro).
Urgent referral ifSymptomatic OR ≥30 mmHg above 95th %ile → same-day ED/urgent eval.
Confirmed persistentStart pharmacotherapy promptly — do not delay for lifestyle trial. ABPM + UA, BUN/Cr, lipids, renal US, echo. Follow-up q2–4 wk until controlled → q3–6 mo. Refer nephrology/cardiology as needed.

Indications: Stage 2 HTN · Symptomatic HTN · End-organ damage (LVH, CKD) · Persistent Stage 1 after 6 mo lifestyle trial · CKD or DM

BP target: <90th %ile (<130/80 if ≥13 yr)

Lifestyle — first-line at all stages
  • →DASH-type diet + sodium restriction
  • →30–60 min moderate-to-vigorous activity daily
  • →Weight management (5–10% loss often normalizes BP in overweight children)
  • →Limit screen time, avoid energy drinks and caffeine
Workup — when to order
  • →Both arms + 1 leg BP once HTN suspected (rule out coarctation)
  • →ABPM — gold standard to confirm; differentiates white-coat vs masked vs sustained HTN
  • →Labs: UA, BUN/Cr, electrolytes, lipid panel ± fasting glucose
  • →Renal US: children <6 yr or abnormal UA/Cr
  • →Echocardiogram: assess for LVH — if present, treat regardless of stage
Common causes by age
InfantsRenal artery stenosis, coarctation of aorta, congenital renal disease
ChildrenRenal parenchymal disease (most common overall), renovascular HTN
AdolescentsPrimary (essential) HTN becomes increasingly common; secondary still possible

Children <6 yr with HTN should be evaluated for secondary causes.

Clinical pearls
  • →Most elevated BP in children is transient (stress, anxiety, improper cuff, white-coat effect)
  • →Confirm diagnosis across ≥3 separate visits before labeling Stage 1 HTN
  • →Tall children have higher "normal" BP ranges — always use height-based percentiles
  • →LVH on echo = treat regardless of BP stage
  • →Diastolic HTN and nocturnal HTN on ABPM → more likely secondary cause
  • →Prepubertal child with HTN → secondary cause more likely; postpubertal → primary more likely
  • →Avoid over-testing early unless red flags: symptoms, very high BP, abnormal exam
  • →Child seated ≥5 min, back supported, feet flat, right arm at heart level
  • →Cuff width = 40% of arm circumference; bladder length = 80–100% of arm circumference
  • →Oscillometric device acceptable for screening; confirm any elevated reading with auscultatory method
  • →Take 2 readings, average them. Repeat on a separate visit before diagnosing HTN
